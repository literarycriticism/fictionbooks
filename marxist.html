MARXIST READING OF F. SCOTT FITZGERALD’S “THE GREAT GATSBY”

F. Scott Fitzgerald's The Great Gatsby is an incredible story that takes place during the Jazz Age, a time when people are obsessed with wealth, partying, and chasing their dreams. The book talks about important things like class, love, and the idea of the American Dream, which is basically the belief that anyone can be happy and successful if they work hard enough. But the story shows that life is way more complicated than that. It’s not just about living a perfect life. It was also about the struggles that people face and the mistakes they make while chasing their goals.

The main character was Jay Gatsby, he was a mysterious guy that who seems to have it all. He always throws these huge and fancy parties at his gigantic mansion that everyone wants to be there. But deep down, Gatsby isn’t really happy. He was in love with this girl named Daisy Buchanan, who he fell in love with a years ago. Gatsby thinks that if he becomes rich and successful then Daisy will fall in love again and be back with him. He almost spend his whole life trying to impress her and even pretending to be someone that he is not. It was kind of sad because no matter how hard he tries, he can’t go back to the past or make Daisy love him the way he wants her to.

The book also talks a lot about how unfair society can be. Like for example, Daisy was stuck in a bad marriage with Tom Buchanan, who doesn’t even treat her well or better. But because it’s the year 1920s, she doesn’t really have the freedom or have a choice to leave him and live her own life. It shows how women back then were expected to just stay in their roles and couldn’t do much about it. And at the same time, you will see how rich people like Tom and Daisy show how they are super selfish and only care about money and appearances. They don’t really think about how their actions gonna affect the others which is why everything in the story falls apart.

One of the biggest messages in the book is that how the American Dream isn’t always what it seems. Gatsby works so hard to become rich and successful, thinking it will make him happy, but in the end, it really doesn’t make him happy at all. His dream was being with Daisy ends up on destroying him, and it shows how chasing money and status can mess people up just like that. The book also makes you think about how people like Gatsby, who start from nothing and

need to have to work way harder than people who are born rich just like Tom and Daisy and to even get close to the same level of success.

In conclusion, "The Great Gatsby" was about in more than just parties and love story of Gatsby and Daisy. It was really deep and to look at how people’s dreams and goals can sometimes lead to heartbreak and failure. Especially, when they were based on things like money and appearances, even though the story is set in the 1920s, it was super relatable because it talks about things like wanting to be happy, struggling and forcing themselves just to fit in their society, and realizing that life doesn’t always turn out in the way what you want it to be. It was a quite sad but a powerful book that makes you think about what really matters in life

- Gutierrez, Anne M.

MARXIST READING OF F. SCOTT FRITZGERALD’S “THE GREAT GATSBY”

The Great Gatsby is quite possibly one of the best American classics written showcasing love, wealth and the American dream. The plot revolves around the character of Jay Gatsby, a self-made millionaire, in his desperate attempt to win over his lost love, Daisy Buchanan, and to recreate a past which can only be remembered, but not replicated. The narrator of the story is Nick Carraway, a young man from the Midwest who traveled all the way to Long Island in search of the American Dream. Nick’s viewpoint has an odd twist to it as it is often ironic when coupled with events that are taking chances in his environment. He is both dispassionate and sentimental, which explains why people reading the book are able to understand both the beauty and the empty nature of Gatsby’s world.

At the heart of Gatsby's downfall, Fitzgerald paints an unflinching portrait of the glory and the failures of the society of the time and so it’s fair to say that the book is a commentary on the disconnect that many Americans experienced post War One. The setting of the novel is the war aftermath which people like Nick, Gatsby, and Tom as the characters of the novel have also fought in. The fiction reflects on the trauma and disruption which most soldiers have to deal with after having been through such life changing experience of fighting in a war. Reasons that critique the Great Gatsby will be discussed, and its pertinency and multifaceted nature will be clarified To Gatsby, the green light is located across the bay, which is an intense source of hope for him for Daisy and his dream which is far out of his reach. The ashes valley, a waste land Military veteran and the unconventional really mean the chaos and the malady experienced by many veterans when coming back to a new America.

The critique will pursue that through The Great Gatsby filtered through various critical lenses, all in an attempt to demonstrate how the unconventional remains relevant and complex. The green light that shines across the bay and into Gatsby's mansion embodies his intentions and dreams of Daisy, that is, the unattainable dream. The valley of ashes, a parched wasteland sprawled between West Egg and New York City, represents the ethical and spiritual decay of American culture. Apart from the theme of Gatsby's wealthy parties, where alcohol flows freely, much hypocrisy and blatant disregard for law during Prohibition period (that is 1920-1933, when the federal government was trying to make an alcoholic ban in the United States) forms a huge aspect of the novel. It also portrays the excess and materialism synonymous with the Jazz Age, for instance, in the opulent lives of people like Gatsby but also the haughty behaviors of many

guests.

Gatsby's obsession with Daisy might have been the desire to recover some lost innocence and an unachievable past. The golden image of Daisy has developed from a very romanticized memory, and thus he never acknowledges the real woman that she has become. The valley of ashes highly contrasts the rich and poor and serves as an apt reminder of the social and economic divide between the affluent and the working class. The poor and despair-stricken residents of the valley, such as George Wilson, are caught in a generational cycle while the wealthy characters on Long Island create an enviable luxury. The Great Gatsby shines bright and continues to be a marvelous novel relevant to the core of contemporary man. through the most essential issues of wealth and love with the lens of the American Dream, Fitzgerald gives an extensive belief The materialism that defined the Jazz Age is vividly illustrated by Gatsby's lavish lifestyle and the superficiality of many of his guests.
