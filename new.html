New Criticism of F. Scott Fritzgerald’s “The Great Gatsby”

In The Great Gatsby, the key element that is most effectively utilized is a captivating point of view. Specifically, Nick's role as an unreliable yet seemingly somewhat rational narrator is misleading and introduces another dimension to the text that complicates it, making it significantly more intriguing than a fully impartial narrative. Notably, Nick's own self-aggrandizement as being "well-rounded," "honest,” and generally moral seems to falter in numerous instances. By the conclusion of the book, Jordan, who candidly acknowledges at times that she is careless, suggests that Nick was also irresponsible and that their relationship was a mishap. Furthermore, this connects to the broader theme of the novel while also depicting Nick as one of those who belongs to the old wealthy class, even though he may not have been born into an extremely affluent family (or perhaps he was, and he minimizes it to create distance from the careless elite he encountered in New York). Overall, he perceives his character as non-judgmental. He dedicates the initial two pages to discussing how his father (who embodies the Midwestern values he believes himself to possess) advised him never to judge others because Nick had been rather fortunate in his circumstances, yet Nick is perpetually evaluating people and portraying situations through a very specific lens. In fact, the entirety of the novel is fundamentally Nick critiquing the East and the wealthy for being morally corrupt.

Another intriguing outcome of Nick’s perspective is the cynically romantic interpretation of the world, particularly regarding Gatsby, which often appears insincere. He seems to admire Gatsby while also criticizing the lifestyle that he embodies. Overall, his perception of Gatsby seems remarkably favorable, yet it is clear that Nick overlooks numerous aspects of Gatsby that he prefers not to consider. For instance, the reality that Gatsby’s wealth is nearly entirely acquired through illegal means seems to disturb Nick surprisingly little, especially for someone who leans towards the law-abiding (or at least professes to) as much as he does. Furthermore, Nick asserts that he loathes the stark disconnection between the affluent and reality, even though he nearly idolizes Gatsby’s idealism. Early in the narrative, when Nick begins to provide an introduction to the book, he starts discussing the reasons for his move back West while also extolling Gatsby. He states that upon returning from the East, he desired everything to be thoroughly moral and lawful, but he makes an exception for Gatsby.

“Only Gatsby, the man who gives his name to this book, was exempt from my reaction—Gatsby, who represented everything for which I have an unaffected scorn. "Curiously, he does not appear to acknowledge his hypocrisy or the reality that he is being partial regarding Gatsby’s actual value, both of which would have been entirely reasonable, but rather seems to believe that, for some inexplicable reason, Gatsby is virtuous despite his imperfections, which embody much of what Nick despised about the East. The inclusion of the quote almost appears as if it was meant to indicate to the reader that Nick would not serve as an unbiased narrator as he claimed.

- Verbo, Jhonn Relly





New Criticism Reading of Lewis Carroll’s “Alice’s Adventure in Wonderland”

Alice’s Adventure in Wonderland written by Lewis Carroll in 1865 is a story about Alice who falls down a rabbit hole and lands into a fantasy world that is full of weird, wonderful people and animals. In the story, she is a bright girl who acts bravely, trusting her gut and the other characters, and refusing to back down when she believes something is absurd. However, she also experiences anxiety and homesickness when she becomes confused and disoriented in Wonderland. She is about to grow up, and Wonderland's experiences play on her fears and show how she is both excited and afraid of the future despite her desire to learn more. The story's protagonist is Alice. Alice has an intense interest in the world around her and thinks everything is stable and neat.

Alice's search to get home after getting lost in a mysterious place is the main source of conflict in Alice's Adventures in Wonderland. Throughout the story, as a little girl, her main objective is to find her way back via Wonderland, a place full of confusing people and circumstances. Like Dorothy in The Wonderful Wizard of Oz, Alice's journey is fundamentally one of survival, with her youth and confusion leading to her difficulties and turning her return home into more than just an urgent but also a survival issue.

Alice finds herself at the croquet match of the Queen of Hearts as she keeps making her way through Wonderland. Known for having little patience, the Queen is angry at Alice and immediately orders her to be killed. As Alice confronts the terrifying possibility of losing her head, the other Wonderland figures let out a terrified gasp. The Queen of Spades steps in to save Alice just as the Queen's guards are about to fulfill the order, stating that she would not tolerate acts of violence in her kingdom. Alice is left safe but terrified as the Queen of Hearts, angered at being stopped, walks off in anger. Alice becomes overwhelmed with relief and appreciation for the Queen of Spades' courage as the other characters applaud her newlyfound ally.

Additionally, the actual world and a fictional dream world alternate throughout Alice in Wonderland. Alice is in the real world, probably in the fall, on the banks of a river. She travels through Wonderland in her dream, running into different people and having adventures. The imaginative aspect of her adventure is emphasized by the fact that Wonderland's events appear to occur in a single afternoon or morning. Thus, growing up, or childhood and adulthood, is the most evident subject throughout Alice's Adventures in Wonderland. However, the story's characters, plot, and setting all work together to make the whole thing more imaginative and well-developed. The difficulties and issues flowed naturally in their resolution.

In conclusion, accepting who you really are is among the life lessons I've learned from Alice’s Adventure in Wonderland. Alice battles the limitations and expectations society places on her. She discovers how to value her individual traits and abilities as she makes her way through Wonderland's absurd and uncertain environment. It's important that we accept and value our individuality and strengths in our own lives. We can achieve happiness in our lives and increase our confidence and self-esteem by accepting ourselves as we are. Surrounding ourselves with people who value and accept us for who we are and encourage us on our path to self-discovery is also crucial.

- Pelaez, Maria Judylyn

New Criticism of The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald

The Great Gatsby written by F. Scott Fitzgerald is a story about how an American dream is not always possible for everyone. Jay Gatsby, the main character, is a rich man who is desperately trying to win the love of his life back, Daisy Buchanan. The story is focused on how Gatsby is trying to recreate his perfect life with Daisy, without fully realizing that times have changed. Although that is the case, Gatsby is still obsessed with wanting Daisy back but struggles to fit into the higher class world that Daisy lives in. Unfortunately, his attempt to win Daisy back and fulfill his dream led to his tragic death.

aGatsby was determined to move on from his previous poor life and become great through wealth. That way, by being rich, Daisy will choose him over her husband, Tom. The only problem was that no matter how rich he gets, he cannot change the past nor fit into the high class society that Daisy is a part of. This part of the story shows how much Gatsby has struggled.

Although the theme of the story revolves around the pursuit of the American dream, it only led to ultimate failure. Gatsby becomes rich, but he becomes rich through illegal activities hoping that it would finally be the reason for him to be accepted into society. Nevertheless, besides him finally becoming rich, he still cannot gain what he desires the most, which is Daisy and to fit into her world. This shows how being rich cannot always lead to true happiness. The American dream can lead anyone to success through hard work, but the author, Fitzgerald, showed that this belief can lead to emptiness by making a story about how Gatsby’s dream failed even though he became rich.

The setting, however, helps explain its theme. The story is in the 1920s, which is a time of wealth but lack of values. The locations—East Egg, West Egg, and the Valley of Ashes—shows the social differences in the story. The East Egg is where the old money lives, like Tom and Daisy. The West Egg, on the other hand, is where the new money lives, like Gatsby, because despite all the money he got, he still is not fully accepted by the old money people, including Daisy. The Valley of Ashes is where the poor, lifeless people live, showing struggles and emptiness behind the rich people's lives.

The structure of the story highlights the theme of illusion versus reality. The story is narrated by Nick Carraway, who is a part of both events and an observer. Nick has a limited perspective, he does not know everything about gatsby right away. However, besides the fact that he has a limited perspective, he is still able to learn small details about Gatsby over time, which makes Gatsby mysterious and seem like an illusion. The way the story is narrated, using flashbacks and hints about the future, makes it clear that Gatsby’s dream of having his perfect life back with Daisy will never happen

In summary, The Great Gatsby shows that the pursuit of gaining wealth and status only leads to emptiness and failure because money does not buy true happiness. Gatsby’s problems, failing to win over Daisy, and the social differences in the setting highlights the story’s main idea. The story is told using Nick’s point of view, which makes Gatsby's dream even more tragic. The author, Fitzgerald, shows how an American dream that is focused only on money and status instead of true happiness can ruin your life.

- Salazar, Riana Althea

Book the First: The Bad Beginning is the first novel of the children's novel series A Series of Unfortunate Events by Lemony Snicket.that was published on September 30, 1999, written by Daniel Handler and illustrated by Brett Helquist. The novel tells the story of three children, Violet, Klaus, and Sunny Baudelaire, who suddenly become orphans following a fire that killed their parents and are sent to live with Count Olaf, who attempts to steal their inheritance

The setting of the book in which the baudelaire lives is very relevant to the story as it matches their transition from a very wealthy lifestyle into a poor and miserable life. The house of Count Olaf can be used as a symbol of how poorly the situations the children are in. The theme of the book is about the importance of knowledge and resourcefulness when facing a crisis; this story also showed the importance of resilience and having actual family and friends help.

The protagonists of the story are the three extraordinarily intelligent siblings, Violet, Klaus, and Sunny. Throughout they showcase their skills as they try to settle down in their new lifestyle in the care of their so-called uncle Count Olaf, who only after their wealth. As they partake in their new journey and face many challenges, they try to use their extraordinary skills to protect themselves and their inheritance in the hands of Count Olaf and his troupe.
